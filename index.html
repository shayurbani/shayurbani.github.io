The Case Against Police Body Cameras

The Brooklyn Chapter of the NAACP says New York City's police body camera program isn't transparent enough. Meanwhile, recent studies cast doubt on the technology's effectiveness.

By: Shay Urbani

One third of police departments in the United States, including the NYPD, have either adopted body cameras or are implementing body camera pilot programs, a technology that many policy makers are hoping is the answer to addressing police misconduct and increasing accountability. But some experts and community members are skeptical.

“What they’re doing, we believe, is not appropriate,” —Kumar Rao

The NYPD pilot for body cameras, which began last April as the direct result of a class action NYC residents filed against the police department for their practice of stop and frisk, has so far rolled out to 14 police precincts with the intent of equipping all officers with cameras by the end of 2019.

At a town hall and panel discussion hosted by the Brooklyn chapter of the NAACP last week, Kumar Rao, senior staff attorney for racial justice at the Center for Popular Democracy, said that although the body camera program was ordered as a way to improve relations between police officers and the communities they serve, the current program policy is deeply problematic. “What they’re doing, we believe, is not appropriate,” Rao said. “It’s not going to advance any of the reasons why they had to do the body camera proposal in the first place, which was to increase transparency, increase accountability, and to create a situation where we can have safer interactions between police and citizens.”

"There are times when an officer made an honest mistake and he thought a civilian being attacked was more important than turning on the camera,” —Sergeant Rudy Hall

One of the complaints that surfaced during the town hall was that the current policy gave too much discretionary power to officers to decide when they activated their cameras, and not enough discipline for officers who failed to comply with the activation guidelines. Sergeant Rudy Hall of the NYPD Compliance Division and adjunct professor at the John Jay College of Criminal Justice, however, said that a strict disciplinary just does not make sense. "There are times when an officer made an honest mistake and he thought a civilian being attacked was more important than turning on the camera,” said Sergeant Hall. “Technically he violated our policy by not turning on the camera first, but I think that’s an honest mistake that most people can understand.

Under the current body camera policy, officers are instructed to record the following situations: all uses of force, arrests, and property searches; when responding to a crime in progress; when patrolling inside a New York Housing Authority building; when transporting persons to a police station, hospital, or jail; and when interacting with “emotionally disturbed” persons. Officers are also allowed to record anytime they believe it would be useful, and they are encouraged, but not required, to inform individuals that recording is in progress

Jamal Akbar, Chair of the Criminal Justice Committee of the Brooklyn NAACP, says that the organization's current stance is that “the policies do not represent the needs of the community” in terms of officer discretion to activate the cameras. In an official statement, the chapter encourages the NYPD to adopt cameras that are motion activated by external stimuli and contain signals that automatically advise the public when they begin recording.
